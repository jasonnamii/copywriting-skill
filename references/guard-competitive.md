# Guard: Competitive Copy Analysis Protocol

**Core Principle:** You cannot differentiate blindly. Competitive copy tracking reveals what others are claiming, what words they own, and what gaps exist. Regular audits are your early-warning system for positioning erosion.

---

## Why Ongoing Competitive Copy Tracking Matters

### The 3 Risks of Ignoring Competitors

1. **Positioning Collapse**
   - You own "fast" ... until 5 competitors claim the same
   - Your headline "No setup required" becomes table stakes (everyone says it)
   - Suddenly your copy sounds generic instead of differentiated

2. **Audience Confusion**
   - If 3 direct competitors use similar language, audience can't distinguish you
   - Your differentiation erodes without you realizing it
   - Market window closes (positioning gets crowded)

3. **Missed Opportunities**
   - While you repeat old messaging, competitors claim emotional benefits you haven't touched
   - They find new audience segments; you stay focused on old ones
   - You're always a quarter behind

### What Tracking Enables

- Early detection: "Competitor just claimed accessibility; we own this. Ship our accessibility story now."
- Differentiation gaps: "Everyone claims 'ease.' What do we claim that they don't?"
- Tone shifts: "Competitor moved from corporate to casual. Should we? Data first."
- New angles: "They're messaging to designers. We haven't touched that segment."

## Competitor Identification Framework

### Tier 1: Direct Competitors (Same Category, Same Audience)

**Definition:** Same product category, targeting same customer

**Examples (Design Tools):**
- Figma → Direct competitors: Adobe XD, Sketch, Framer, Penpot
- Linear → Direct competitors: Jira, Asana, Monday.com
- Notion → Direct competitors: Obsidian, Roam Research, Evernote

**Tracking Frequency:** Monthly (things change fast)

**Why:** They're fighting for same customer budget. Their copy is most likely to erode your positioning.

### Tier 2: Indirect Competitors (Same Audience, Different Category)

**Definition:** Solve same customer pain in different way; audience might choose them instead of you

**Examples:**
- Figma (design) vs Webflow (no-code website builder) — both target designers, different tools
- Linear (task management) vs Slack (communication) — both target teams, different function
- Notion (all-in-one) vs specialized tools (Figma, Slack, Linear) — competes for workflows, not function

**Tracking Frequency:** Quarterly (shifts are slower)

**Why:** These competitors shape audience expectations and alternatives. Understanding how they position changes helps you defend against "why use yours vs theirs" objections.

### Tier 3: Aspirational Competitors (Tone Benchmark, Not Direct Threat)

**Definition:** Not competing for customers, but you admire their copy/brand; they set a tone standard

**Examples:**
- Technical tool → benchmarks against Apple or Stripe (aspirational tone)
- B2B SaaS → benchmarks against Mailchimp or Slack (copy quality bar)
- Design tool → benchmarks against Nike or Patagonia (brand voice)

**Tracking Frequency:** Quarterly (tone changes slowly)

**Why:** Helps you understand what "excellent copy in your space" looks like. Sets floor for your ambition.

## Copy Audit Analysis Framework

### Layer 1: Positioning Comparison

**For each competitor, extract:**
- What word/phrase do they "own" (claim repeatedly)?
- What's their primary benefit claim?
- What's their secondary claim?

| Competitor | Primary Claim | Secondary Claim | Own Word |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| Figma | "Figma brings tools, teams, and your community into one space" | Collaboration, design system management | "space" (community) |
| Adobe XD | "Professional tools for digital design" | Professional-grade, integration | "professional" |
| Framer | "The web builder for creative pros" | No-code web building, design-to-code | "creative" |
| Penpot | "The open-source design and prototyping platform" | Open source, collaboration, transparency | "open" |

**Decision:** What do we claim that they don't? (Opportunity) What do they all claim that we don't? (Commodity risk)

### Layer 2: Tone Mapping (4 Axes)

Map each competitor on these 4 axes:

**Axis 1: Formal ↔ Casual**
- Figma: Casual (uses contractions, friendly voice)
- Adobe XD: Formal (professional, B2B tone)

**Axis 2: Emotional ↔ Rational**
- Figma: Emotional (emphasizes community, creativity)
- Linear: Rational (speed metrics, efficiency focus)

**Axis 3: Playful ↔ Serious**
- Slack: Very playful (emojis, jokes in copy)
- Jira: Serious (enterprise, no-nonsense)

**Axis 4: Aspirational ↔ Pragmatic**
- Nike: Aspirational (inspiration, self-improvement)
- IKEA: Pragmatic (value, functionality)

**Tone Map Visualization:**

```
       Formal
         |
Rational |----------| Emotional
         |
       Casual

Direct competitors (Figma, Adobe XD, Framer):
- Figma: Casual + Emotional (upper right)
- Adobe XD: Formal + Rational (upper left)
- Framer: Casual + Emotional (upper right, slightly closer to rational)

Our position: Where should we sit?
- If we occupy same spot as Figma (casual + emotional), we lose.
- If we occupy formal + rational (Adobe XD), we're a different persona.
- Differentiation: Move to rational + casual (efficient but friendly).
```

### Layer 3: Gap Identification

**What are they NOT saying that we COULD?**

| Gap | Example | Our Opportunity |
|-----|---------|-----------------|
| Emotional benefit absent | No one mentions "design gives you confidence" | Own emotional angle |
| Audience segment unclaimed | Everyone targets "teams"; no one targets "solo creators" | Target solo creator segment |
| Functional benefit ignored | Everyone mentions speed; no one mentions "error prevention" | Own reliability angle |
| Value claim missing | No one mentions "save money," just "save time" | Economic angle |
| Sustainability unclaimed | (If you care) No one mentions environmental | Green/ethical angle |

**Decision Framework:**
- Gap = real opportunity if: (a) audience cares about it, (b) we can credibly own it, (c) no one else is there
- Gap = red herring if: audience doesn't care, or we can't credibly claim it

## Differentiation Rules: How to Be Different Without Being Contrarian for Its Own Sake

### Rule 1: Differentiate on a Real Dimension, Not Invented Ones

**Do:**
- "We're 10x faster" (if you have benchmark data)
- "We're the only open-source option" (if true)
- "We focus on solo creators" (if you've chosen that segment)

**Don't:**
- "We're more beautiful" (subjective, hard to prove)
- "We're more ethical" (claim everyone makes)
- "We're the underdog" (everyone says this; no differentiation)

### Rule 2: Don't Contradict Audience Expectations

**Anti-pattern:**
- Everyone in category says "Easy to use." You say "Complex, but worth it."
- Audience doesn't buy your product to be challenged. They buy it to solve problems.
- Contradiction = confusing, not differentiating

**Better approach:**
- Everyone says "Easy." You say "Easy + Powerful" or "Easy for beginners, powerful for pros"
- Additive differentiation (not contradictory)

### Rule 3: Differentiate on What Matters to Your Audience, Not What Matters to You

**Anti-pattern:**
- You're proud of your code architecture. You emphasize "modular, well-engineered codebase."
- Audience doesn't care about code. They care about: "works reliably," "ships fast," "no surprises."

**Better approach:**
- Translate your strength into audience benefit: "Built to scale without breaking. Works reliably, month one."

### Rule 4: Differentiate Where Your Competitors Are Weak

**Analysis:**
- All direct competitors claim speed. You are equally fast.
- All direct competitors claim ease. You are equally easy.
- But no one claims "You don't have to learn a new workflow. Works with your Figma skills."

**Your differentiation:** Compatibility + transition ease

### Rule 5: Don't Differentiate on Everything

**Anti-pattern:**
- You try to be different on: speed, ease, price, design, collaboration, integrations, support, philosophy
- You sound incoherent; audience doesn't know why to pick you

**Better approach:**
- Pick 1-2 dimensions where you're genuinely different
- On everything else, match expectations (don't claim, don't undersell, just meet standard)

## C8 Competitive Landscape (Creator Tool Copy)

### Direct Competitors in Creator Space

| Tool | Primary Position | Tone | Audience | Differentiation |
|------|------------------|------|----------|-----------------|
| **Figma** | "Collaborative design platform" | Casual, community-focused | Design teams | Accessibility, open ecosystem |
| **Framer** | "Design and ship web projects" | Casual, builder-focused | Designers who code | Design-to-code automation |
| **Notion** | "All your tools in one" | Casual, personality | Knowledge workers | Flexibility, customization |
| **Linear** | "Issue tracking that scales" | Direct, minimalist | Dev teams | Elegant UX, CLI-native |
| **Slack** | "Where work happens" | Playful, cultural | Teams | Ubiquity, integration hub |
| **Arc Browser** | "The browser for power users" | Playful, technical | Developer audience | Speed, keyboard-native |

### Key Positioning Trends (C8)

1. **All moving casual/playful** (formal is out)
2. **All emphasizing "creator economy"** (but most don't target creators authentically)
3. **All claiming "built for you"** (watch for cliché — everyone says it)
4. **Most avoid superlatives** (smart; avoids backlash)
5. **Most use emoji in social** (visual, fun signal)

### C8-Specific Copy Patterns

**What's working:**
- Short, punchy headlines (5-8 words)
- Specific benefits, not abstract claims ("Edit videos faster" not "Optimize your workflow")
- Creator-specific language (algorithm, drop, collab, vibe, energy)
- Honesty about product reality ("No magic, just better tools")
- Insider jokes (requires deep audience knowledge)

**What's NOT working:**
- "For creators" tagline (everyone says it; means nothing)
- "Join the community" (vague, sounds corporate)
- "AI-powered" (unless demonstrably useful; sounds like hype)
- Overly formal tone (contradicts "creator-focused" claim)
- Generic inspiration (feels inauthentic to creator audience)

### Competitive Gaps in Creator Space

| Gap | Why It Matters | Opportunity |
|-----|----------------|-------------|
| No one talks about boring stuff (reliability, documentation, support) | Creators assume it's bad; if you own it, credibility spike | "Tools that actually work. Real support. No BS." |
| Everyone emphasizes speed; no one emphasizes joy | Creators are burned out; emotional benefit untouched | Own happiness angle |
| Everyone targets teams; no one targets solo creators | Solo creator segment growing; underserved | "Built for you, not your team" |
| Everyone claims "no-code"; no one claims "low-code" | "No-code" has become commodity; low-code is positioning opening | "Code if you want, visual if you don't" |
| No one acknowledges learning curve honestly | Creators expect tools to be hard; if you're upfront, trust spikes | "2-hour learning curve, lifetime value" |

## Red Flags: When to Update Your Positioning

**Update your copy when:**

- [ ] Competitor directly copies your positioning language
- [ ] Two or more competitors claim your differentiator
- [ ] Competitor significantly outspends you in marketing (language saturation)
- [ ] Audience sentiment shifts (social listening shows new priorities)
- [ ] Market entrant uses new positioning you hadn't considered
- [ ] Your claim becomes industry standard (no longer differentiating)

**Example escalation:**
- Month 1: We claim "fast design"
- Month 2: Figma claims "fast design"
- Month 3: Framer claims "fast design"
- Month 4: ACTION REQUIRED — "fast design" is now table stakes, not differentiation

## Final Checkpoint

Before launching any competitive analysis:

1. **Tier assignment:** Is this direct/indirect/aspirational?
2. **Capture completeness:** Do I have homepage, onboarding, email, social samples?
3. **Transcription:** Did I transcribe (not just screenshot)?
4. **Tone mapping:** Can I place each competitor on the 4 axes?
5. **Gap analysis:** What are they NOT claiming that we could?
6. **Differentiation test:** If I claim it, is it: (a) real, (b) valuable to audience, (c) credible?
7. **Quarterly update:** Is my positioning matrix current?

If any fails → gather more data, analyze, then decide if positioning shift needed.
